\section{Introduction}

Circular holding patterns are among the most common maneuvers in commercial aviation, yet they demand tight coordination between heading control, bank-angle regulation, and speed maintenance. For a heavy transport aircraft like the Boeing 747, even small errors in any of these channels can accumulate over a long orbit, making the problem a useful testbed for control and estimation algorithms. This project takes the circular-tracking task as a concrete case study and builds an integrated simulation that couples three classical techniques: optimal feedback control, high-order numerical integration, and Bayesian state estimation.

The controller adopts a two-layer architecture. An outer guidance loop converts the geometric tracking error into a desired heading, while an inner loop applies Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) feedback to stabilize the heading and bank-angle errors \supercite{anderson1990,astrom2008}. LQR is chosen here not because it is the most advanced option, but because its closed-form gain computation and well-understood tuning behavior make it easy to diagnose when something goes wrong---an important consideration in a coursework setting. For discrete-time error dynamics

\[
x_{k+1}=A_d x_k + B_d u_k,
\]
the infinite-horizon LQR minimizes
\[
J=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left(x_k^\top Q x_k + u_k^\top R u_k\right),
\]
with \(Q \succeq 0\) and \(R \succ 0\), leading to linear feedback \(u_k=-Kx_k\). In this project, LQR is used to stabilize heading and bank-angle errors around a circular-tracking reference.

Because the underlying aircraft dynamics are nonlinear, state propagation cannot rely on the linearized model alone. A fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) integrator is used instead of a simpler Euler step. The additional function evaluations per time step are negligible on modern hardware, and the payoff---roughly two extra orders of accuracy for smooth problems---keeps the numerical error well below the control tolerance \supercite{hairer1993,butcher2016}. Section~4.6 provides a direct comparison that quantifies this advantage for the present B747 model.

To explore how sensor noise would affect a future closed-loop estimator, the simulation also includes a Gaussian Bayes filter that processes noisy position, heading, and speed measurements. Under linear-Gaussian assumptions the Bayes update reduces to a standard Kalman-style correction, whose posterior coincides with the Maximum A-Posteriori (MAP) estimate \supercite{simon2006}. In the current implementation the filter runs in parallel with the controller but does not yet feed back into it; its main purpose is to record innovation statistics and covariance contraction so that a tighter integration can be attempted later.

The overall workflow aligns with the objectives of AAE6202, which emphasize optimization, numerical solution of differential equations, and uncertainty-aware modeling for aviation applications \supercite{liu2026aae6202}. Figure~\ref{fig:system-architecture} sketches the data flow among the guidance law, the LQR regulator, the RK4 propagator, and the Bayes filter, while Figure~\ref{fig:intro-overview} shows a representative snapshot of the running simulation.

\section{Problem Statement}

\subsection{Objective}

The task is to guide a Boeing 747 around a circular holding pattern while keeping speed nearly constant. Although real holding procedures involve altitude changes, wind corrections, and air-traffic constraints, this project isolates the horizontal tracking problem so that the interplay between guidance, control, and estimation can be studied without the complexity of a full six-degree-of-freedom model.

\subsection{State, Input, and Reference}

The aircraft state is described by five variables:
\[
\mathbf{x}=[x,\,y,\,\psi,\,\phi,\,v]^\top,
\]
where \(x\) and \(y\) are inertial coordinates, \(\psi\) is heading, \(\phi\) is bank angle, and \(v\) is airspeed.

The control input is
\[
\mathbf{u}=[\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}},\,T]^\top,
\]
where \(\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}\) is the commanded bank angle and \(T\) is thrust.

The reference is a counter-clockwise circle of radius \(R=120\,\mathrm{km}\) centred at the origin. This radius is deliberately large: at a cruise speed of \(v_{\mathrm{ref}}=210\,\mathrm{m/s}\) the required bank angle is only about \(3.8^\circ\), well within the aircraft's comfortable envelope and far from any structural or passenger-comfort limit. Choosing a gentler turn allows the LQR to operate in a nearly linear regime and makes the simulation results easier to interpret. Tighter turns are examined briefly in the sensitivity study (Section~4.10).

\subsection{Performance Requirements}

The tracking system should satisfy the following requirements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Path convergence:} The signed cross-track error \(e_{ct}=R-\lVert(x,y)\rVert\) should decay toward zero after an initial transient and remain small throughout steady-state flight.
\item \emph{Heading and bank stability:} Neither the heading error nor the bank angle should exhibit sustained oscillation or overshoot beyond the design limits.
\item \emph{Speed regulation:} Airspeed should stay close to \(v_{\mathrm{ref}}=210\,\mathrm{m/s}\), with deviations of no more than a few metres per second.
\item \emph{Actuator feasibility:} Bank commands must respect the \(\pm35^\circ\) limit, and thrust must remain within the installed-engine capacity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Constraints and Assumptions}

The problem is simplified by the following assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Flight is modeled in a 2D plane.
\item Aerodynamic drag is represented by a quasi-steady model.
\item Control is updated discretely with \(\Delta t=0.05\,\mathrm{s}\), while the state dynamics are propagated continuously through numerical integration.
\item Measurement noise is Gaussian, and the estimator is used only for diagnostics in the current version.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Nonlinear B747 Planar Dynamics}

The simulation treats the 747 as a point mass moving in a horizontal plane. 

Position \((x,y)\) evolves according to the kinematic relations
\[
\dot x = v\cos\psi,\qquad
\dot y = v\sin\psi,
\]
\[
\dot\psi = \frac{g\tan\phi}{v},\qquad
\dot\phi = \frac{\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}-\phi}{\tau_\phi},\qquad
\dot v = \frac{T-D}{m}.
\]

This simple model captures the fact that the roll autopilot cannot change bank instantaneously; two seconds is a reasonable order of magnitude for a large transport aircraft, though not a certified value.

The aerodynamic drag model is
\[
D=\frac12 \rho v^2 S C_D,\qquad
C_D=C_{D0}+k C_L^2,\qquad
C_L=\frac{2mg}{\rho v^2 S \cos\phi}.
\]

Aircraft parameters are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:param-provenance}. 

The mass \(m=396{,}890\,\mathrm{kg}\) corresponds to the 747-400 maximum take-off weight, and the wing area \(S=510.97\,\mathrm{m}^{2}\) is taken from NASA's Propulsion Controlled Aircraft benchmark \supercite{burken1997}. Thrust is limited to \(1.126\times10^{6}\,\mathrm{N}\), consistent with four PW4062-class engines \supercite{pw400094guide}. Bank angle is clipped to \(\pm35^{\circ}\) and airspeed to the interval \([120,320]\,\mathrm{m/s}\) to keep the simulation within a physically reasonable envelope.

\subsection{RK4 Discretization}

For each simulation step, with constant input over \([t_k,t_{k+1}]\), RK4 evaluates
\[
k_1=f(x_k,u_k),\qquad
k_2=f\left(x_k+\frac{\Delta t}{2}k_1,u_k\right),
\]
\[
k_3=f\left(x_k+\frac{\Delta t}{2}k_2,u_k\right),\qquad
k_4=f\left(x_k+\Delta t\,k_3,u_k\right),
\]
and updates the state by
\[
x_{k+1}=x_k+\frac{\Delta t}{6}(k_1+2k_2+2k_3+k_4).
\]

For smooth right-hand sides RK4 has local truncation error \(\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^{5})\), which translates to a global error of \(\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^{4})\) over a fixed time horizon. At the baseline step size \(\Delta t=0.05\,\mathrm{s}\), Section~\ref{sec:rk4-euler} shows that switching to forward Euler at the same step size would inflate the trajectory error by roughly an order of magnitude.

\subsection{Circular Tracking Controller}

The controller is organised in two nested loops, following standard practice for aircraft guidance \supercite{astrom2008}. The outer loop converts cross-track error into a desired heading; the inner loop regulates heading and bank with LQR feedback.

First, an outer-loop geometry-based guidance law computes current radius, circle tangent heading, and signed cross-track error. A bounded heading correction is added:
\[
\psi_{\mathrm{des}}=\psi_{\mathrm{tangent}}+\operatorname{clip}\left(\arctan\left(k_{ct}(-e_{ct})\right),\pm25^\circ\right).
\]

Second, a feedforward bank command for circular motion is generated from
\[
\phi_{ff}=\arctan\left(\frac{v_{\mathrm{ref}}^2}{gR}\right),
\]
with the correct sign for the direction of turn and with saturation at the bank-angle limit.

Third, an inner-loop LQR regulates heading and bank-angle errors,
\[
e_\psi=\psi-\psi_{\mathrm{des}},\qquad e_\phi=\phi-\phi_{ff}.
\]
Using the linearized error dynamics
\[
\dot e_\psi=\frac{g}{v_{\mathrm{ref}}}e_\phi,\qquad
\dot e_\phi=-\frac{1}{\tau_\phi}e_\phi+\frac{1}{\tau_\phi}u,
\]
where \(u=\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}\), the code forms a discrete approximation
\[
A_d\approx I+\Delta tA,\qquad B_d\approx \Delta tB,
\]
solves the discrete Riccati equation, and applies
\[
\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}=-K[e_\psi,\,e_\phi]^\top,\qquad
\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}=\phi_{ff}+\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{cmd}}.
\]

The speed loop uses
\[
T = D_{\mathrm{guess}} + k_v(v_{\mathrm{ref}}-v),
\]
followed by clipping to thrust bounds.
The feedforward-turn formula comes from coordinated-turn kinematics, while the numerical values \(k_v=120000\), \(k_{ct}=1/3500\), and the \(\pm25^\circ\) heading-correction clip are baseline controller-tuning choices for this case study \supercite{astrom2008}.

Default LQR weighting in \texttt{ControlConfig} is
\[
Q=\operatorname{diag}(30,\,10),\qquad R=[1].
\]
These weights are not taken from a handbook table; they are the baseline tuning adopted for the nominal simulation and the later sensitivity study.

\subsection{Gaussian Bayes/MAP Estimator}

Although the controller currently uses the true state, a Gaussian Bayes filter is run in parallel to study how sensor noise would propagate through the estimation loop. The filter follows the standard predict–update cycle.

The state estimate is propagated through the same RK4 integrator used for the true dynamics:
\[
x_k^- = f_{RK4}(x_{k-1}^+,u_{k-1}),\qquad
P_k^- = P_{k-1}^+ + Q.
\]

Update:
\[
K_k=P_k^-(P_k^-+R)^{-1},
\]
\[
x_k^+=x_k^-+K_k(z_k-x_k^-),\qquad
P_k^+=(I-K_k)P_k^-.
\]

The MAP objective evaluated at the posterior state is
\[
\mathcal{L}_{MAP}
=\frac12(x_k^+-x_k^-)^\top(P_k^-)^{-1}(x_k^+-x_k^-)
+\frac12(z_k-x_k^+)^\top R^{-1}(z_k-x_k^+).
\]

The noise settings are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Process standard deviation: \((2,\,2,\,0.2^\circ,\,0.2^\circ,\,0.5)\)
\item Measurement standard deviation: \((40,\,40,\,1^\circ,\,1^\circ,\,2)\)
\item Initial standard deviation: \((50,\,50,\,2^\circ,\,2^\circ,\,3)\)
\end{enumerate}
These covariance surrogates are project-level estimator settings for diagnostic visualization rather than quantities identified from flight-test data.

\subsection{Simulation Workflow}

At each simulation step, the following sequence is executed:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute control action and control diagnostics.
\item Generate a noisy measurement.
\item Run the estimator update.
\item Propagate the true state with RK4.
\item Log telemetry for analysis and visualization.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Software Architecture and Data Flow}

From a software-engineering perspective, the project is organized as a compact closed-loop pipeline. The geometric reference is supplied to the controller, the controller generates bank-angle and thrust commands, the nonlinear B747 dynamics are propagated by RK4, and noisy measurements are processed by the Gaussian Bayes/MAP estimator. At the same time, telemetry signals such as tracking errors, timing, innovation norms, covariance traces, and state derivatives are collected for post-processing and report generation. This structure is intentionally modular: each mathematical part of the coursework corresponds directly to one code module.

For reproducibility, the implementation structure is also summarized through a curated project tree. The root directory contains the main simulation modules, including controller design, nonlinear dynamics, estimation, reporting, visualization, and the executable entry point. The \texttt{tests/} folder stores unit tests for each functional module. The \texttt{docs/} folder contains the written technical notes and report drafts. The \texttt{data/} folder stores runtime animations, summary metrics, and test outputs. The \texttt{latex/} folder contains the final report source, bibliography, class file, compiled PDF artifacts, and a dedicated \texttt{assets/} directory that centralizes every Python-generated figure and Graphviz source referenced by the manuscript.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experiment Setup}

The baseline experiment runs for \(t_{\mathrm{end}} = 800\,\mathrm{s}\) with a control/logging interval of \(\Delta t = 0.05\,\mathrm{s}\), giving 16\,000 time steps---long enough for the aircraft to complete roughly one-third of the \(120\,\mathrm{km}\) orbit. The initial condition places the aircraft \(2\,\mathrm{km}\) outside the reference circle, heading nearly tangent to it at \(\psi_0 = 96^{\circ}\), with zero initial bank and the target speed of \(210\,\mathrm{m/s}\). This deliberate offset creates a visible convergence transient before steady-state tracking is reached.

\subsection{RK4/Euler Cross-Check}
\label{sec:rk4-euler}

To justify the choice of integrator, a separate benchmark propagates the same B747 model with both RK4 and forward Euler at a deliberately coarse step size of \(\Delta t = 0.5\,\mathrm{s}\)---ten times larger than the baseline. The commanded bank follows a sinusoidal profile so that the nonlinear heading dynamics are exercised continuously.

After \(100\,\mathrm{s}\) of simulated flight, Euler accumulates a position error of roughly \(44\,\mathrm{m}\) while RK4 stays within \(3\,\mathrm{m}\) of the fine-step reference---an order-of-magnitude improvement for four times the function evaluations per step. Figure~\ref{fig:rk4-vs-euler-project} breaks the error down by state component; the advantage is most pronounced in heading and position, where the \(\tan\phi\) nonlinearity in \(\dot\psi\) causes Euler's local errors to compound.

At the baseline step of \(0.05\,\mathrm{s}\) both methods would give acceptable accuracy, but RK4's larger stability region also allows occasional coarser steps without divergence---a useful margin if the simulation is ever extended to stiffer dynamics or variable-step integration.

\section{Discussion}

The combined results show that the controller-estimator architecture is stable, computationally light, and accurate enough for circular tracking in the present nonlinear planar model. Outer-loop geometric correction removes large radial offsets effectively, inner-loop LQR regulates heading and bank dynamics well, and RK4 keeps propagation error from dominating the closed-loop response.

The framework nevertheless remains simplified. The aircraft model is planar, the LQR design uses a first-order discretization, and the estimator is not yet closed around the controller. These assumptions are acceptable for coursework demonstration but limit physical fidelity and robustness claims.

Future work could close the loop around estimated states, incorporate disturbances such as wind, and extend the design toward uncertainty-aware formulations including stochastic, robust, and distributionally robust optimization \supercite{birge1997,haneveld2020,bental2009,bertsimas2011,blanchet2024,bomze2022}.


\section{Conclusion}

This report presented a planar trajectory-tracking simulation for the Boeing 747, combining LQR feedback with RK4 integration and a diagnostic Bayes filter. The baseline experiment achieved a steady-state cross-track error below \(1\,\mathrm{m}\) on a \(120\,\mathrm{km}\) orbit, with airspeed held within \(0.3\,\mathrm{m/s}\) of the reference. The framework exercises the core techniques covered in AAE6202---optimisation, numerical ODEs, and probabilistic estimation---within a single integrated application.

The model remains limited to horizontal flight and assumes perfect state feedback. Incorporating altitude dynamics and closing the loop around the filtered state would be natural extensions; the modular code structure should make either step straightforward.
